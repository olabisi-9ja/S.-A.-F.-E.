In Nigeria, university environments face escalating security threats, yet campus security management largely relies on conventional, reactive physical measures. At Kwara State University (KWASU), the complete absence of a functional digital crime and emergency reporting platform has resulted in chronic underreporting, delayed response times, and a critical communication gap between the university community and security personnel. This research designs and implements the Smart Alert and Field Emergency (S.A.F.E) system, a mobile and web-based security platform tailored for the KWASU environment. The system is built on a three-tier architecture utilising React Native for the mobile client, Node.js and Socket.io for real-time backend communication, and MySQL for data persistence. To address the infrastructural limitation of internet dependency, the system integrates Mesh Networking capabilities — via Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) and Wi-Fi Direct — to enable decentralised, peer-to-peer emergency alerting when cellular networks fail. Furthermore, an Artificial Intelligence (AI) module is embedded to perform intelligent incident classification using a quantised DistilBERT NLP model, automated severity scoring, fake-report detection, and predictive hotspot analytics powered by the DBSCAN clustering algorithm. The completed system was evaluated through unit testing, black-box testing against twelve functional requirements, user acceptance testing with fifty participants, and a formal assessment against the ISO/IEC 25010 software quality framework. All twelve functional requirements were satisfied, mean user acceptance scores exceeded 4.2 out of 5 across all evaluated criteria, emergency alert delivery achieved an average online latency of 1.8 seconds and a Mesh relay latency of 4.2 seconds, and the system maintained 100% alert delivery during simulated network outages. The study concludes that the S.A.F.E system provides Kwara State University with a functional, fault-tolerant, and intelligent digital security infrastructure where none previously existed, and recommends its institutional adoption alongside structured user training and network infrastructure investment.LIST OF TABLESTable 3.1: ISO/IEC 25010 Quality Characteristics and Evaluation FocusTable 4.1: Sprint Implementation SummaryTable 4.2: Database Tables and RelationshipsTable 4.3: REST API EndpointsTable 4.4: Real-Time Socket.io EventsTable 4.5: Sample Unit Test Cases and OutcomesTable 4.6: Black-Box Testing Outcomes Against Functional RequirementsTable 4.7: User Acceptance Testing Results (n = 50)Table 4.8: Evaluation Against the ISO/IEC 25010 Quality FrameworkCHAPTER ONE: INTRODUCTION1.1 Background of the StudySecurity constitutes one of the most fundamental prerequisites for the effective functioning of any university. The capacity of an institution to fulfil its mandate of teaching, learning, and research is inextricably linked to the safety and order that prevails within its environment. In Nigeria, this essential condition is increasingly under serious threat. Across university campuses, incidents of theft, assault, sexual harassment, vandalism, cultism, and armed robbery have become disturbingly commonplace, endangering the physical and psychological wellbeing of students and staff, disrupting academic programmes, and eroding institutional credibility (Awosika et al., 2025).Campus security management in most Nigerian universities continues to rely on conventional physical measures — security guards, foot and vehicular patrols, perimeter fencing, and gate access control. While these measures offer a degree of protective coverage, they are widely acknowledged to be insufficient against the evolving complexity and frequency of modern security threats. Their most critical structural weakness is the absence of any real-time communication and reporting infrastructure: incidents are communicated to security authorities well after they have transpired, substantially diminishing the probability of timely intervention (Wodi &amp; Wordu, 2024).The rapid advancement of Information and Communication Technology (ICT) has created unprecedented opportunities to transform institutional security management. Mobile applications, real-time communication platforms, GPS tracking systems, and data analytics tools have collectively redefined what is achievable in campus security operations. Globally, these technologies are embedded in emergency response infrastructure, enabling rapid incident detection, instantaneous communication, and coordinated multi-agency responses. Yet Ogbu and Excellence (2025) found that emergency alert systems and mobile security applications remain the most underutilised ICT security tools in Nigerian university environments, despite clear evidence of their effectiveness. Lawan and Shrivastava (2023) similarly identified a critical implementation gap, noting that key surveillance and communication technologies remain below optimal deployment levels in Nigerian institutions due to infrastructural constraints, financial limitations, and policy inertia.It is within this context that Kwara State University presents a specific and pressing institutional problem: there is currently no functional digital platform through which members of the university community can report security incidents or receive emergency notifications. Furthermore, existing digital solutions in the broader market often fail in resource-constrained environments due to absolute reliance on stable internet connectivity and a lack of intelligent data processing. This project, the Smart Alert and Field Emergency (S.A.F.E) System, is designed and implemented in direct response to that institutional gap. By integrating Mesh Networking for offline fault tolerance and Artificial Intelligence for intelligent threat triage, the S.A.F.E system provides a resilient, context-aware security infrastructure tailored specifically for the Nigerian university context.1.2 Statement of the ProblemKwara State University currently has no functional digital system for reporting crimes or communicating security emergencies on campus. Students and staff who witness or experience security incidents are compelled to rely on entirely manual mechanisms — physically presenting themselves at a security post, making a telephone call, or waiting for a patrol to pass. These methods are inherently slow, structurally inaccessible during active emergencies, and provide no real-time coordination between incident reporters and security responders.The consequences are well-documented in the literature. Suleiman and Akanbi (2024) identified the absence of structured, accessible reporting channels as a primary cause of chronic underreporting of security incidents in Nigerian universities — a pattern that renders official security data unreliable and prevents evidence-based security planning. Inyang (2025) further observed that the absence of integrated digital security tools leaves security administrations without the situational awareness needed for proactive threat management.Additionally, where digital systems do exist, they suffer from two critical technical deficiencies: they are entirely dependent on continuous internet connectivity — rendering them useless during network blackouts — and they treat all alerts with equal priority, leading to responder fatigue. The absence of an implemented, fault-tolerant, and intelligent solution at this institution is the precise problem this project addresses. The S.A.F.E system is designed and built to replace the current void with a functional, tested, and institutionally appropriate digital security platform.1.3 Aim and Objectives of the StudyAimThe primary aim of this project is to design and implement an AI-powered, mesh-enabled mobile and web-based crime and emergency reporting system for Kwara State University that enables real-time incident reporting, fault-tolerant emergency notification, and efficient, coordinated security response.ObjectivesTo develop a mobile platform through which students and staff can report security incidents instantly from any location on campus.To implement a one-touch emergency alert feature that transmits immediate distress notifications to security personnel, utilising Mesh Networking as a decentralised fallback when internet connectivity is unavailable.To integrate GPS-assisted location identification enabling security personnel to accurately locate and respond to reported incidents.To build a two-way communication interface facilitating direct, documented interaction between incident reporters and security administrators throughout the incident management lifecycle.To develop a centralised administrative dashboard enhanced with Artificial Intelligence for real-time incident monitoring, automated threat classification, predictive hotspot analysis, and data visualisation.To evaluate the system against the ISO/IEC 25010 software quality framework across the dimensions of functional suitability, usability, reliability, security, and performance efficiency.1.4 Significance of the StudyPractically, the system provides Kwara State University with a digital security infrastructure that has not previously existed — enabling faster incident reporting, reducing communication delays, and improving the capacity of security personnel to respond to threats in real time. As Yusuf, Oniye, and Abdulrasaq (2022) established, accessible alerting mechanisms produce measurable reductions in incident severity; this system extends that proven principle into a comprehensive digital platform.Technologically, the project demonstrates in a concrete and replicable manner how advanced ICT tools — specifically Edge AI and decentralised Mesh Networking — can be integrated into an institutional security framework to achieve measurable improvements. The centralised incident data generated by the system equips security administrators with the intelligence needed for pattern recognition, hotspot analysis, and strategic resource allocation.Academically, this project contributes a functional, tested system to the scholarly literature on digital security in the Nigerian educational context — a domain in which, as Wodi and Wordu (2024) observed, empirically evaluated implementations remain rare.1.5 Scope of the StudyThis project covers the design, development, and prototype implementation of the S.A.F.E crime and emergency reporting system, scoped specifically to the operational environment of Kwara State University. REVIEW2.1 IntroductionThis chapter presents a systematic and critical review of existing scholarly literature relevant to the design and implementation of the S.A.F.E system. The review is structured to examine the conceptual foundations of security and insecurity in educational institutions; the role of ICT in contemporary security management; digital technologies and security systems; security management in higher education; the communication gap in campus security; emergency response technologies; and the challenges of ICT implementation in Nigerian institutional contexts. Through this synthesis, substantive research gaps are identified, each of which directly informs and justifies the design of the proposed system and its AI and Mesh Networking enhancements.2.2 Concept of Security and InsecuritySecurity, in its broadest conceptual sense, denotes a state of freedom from threat, danger, or harm — encompassing the protection of human lives, physical property, and institutional integrity from both internal and external sources of disruption. Within the context of higher education, security implies the maintenance of an environment sufficiently safe and orderly to permit the unimpeded pursuit of academic objectives. In the Nigerian context, insecurity manifests across a broad and deeply troubling range of forms, from property crimes to person-directed offences. Awosika, Austin, Ikusika, Mbaegbu, and Okafor-Agbala (2025) identified political instability, ethnic tensions, poverty, unemployment, and corruption as the most persistent structural drivers of insecurity in Nigerian tertiary institutions. Abdulsalam, Buhari, Babarinde, and Adisa (2023) argued that effective responses must combine technological intervention with structural reforms targeting the root causes of insecurity. These structural drivers constitute the backdrop against which the S.A.F.E system is deployed: ICT-based platforms are a necessary but not alone sufficient response.2.3 Role of ICT in Security ManagementThe integration of ICT into security management has become one of the most consequential developments in contemporary security practice. ICT facilitates real-time surveillance, instantaneous multi-channel communication, automated data capture, advanced analytical processing, and the coordination of distributed response networks. Lawan and Shrivastava (2023) found that ICT-based interventions significantly enhance the capacity of security agencies to protect lives and property, though deployment remains below optimal levels due to infrastructural constraints. Abdullahi and Muhammad (2025) demonstrated the powerful capabilities of geospatial technologies for crime mapping and spatial pattern analysis. Their findings directly inform the S.A.F.E system's GPS-assisted location tagging and AI-driven predictive mapping features.2.4 Digital Technology and Security SystemsDigital technology fundamentally reshapes security management from reactive models to proactive, data-driven frameworks. Adeoluwa, Adedayo, Okoye, Badmus, Adeoluwa, and Adabembe (2025) provided empirical evidence that digital security tools significantly enhance the capacity to identify threats and maintain post-incident accountability, enabling a paradigm shift toward intelligence-led governance. Mustapha, Emmanuel, and Danjuma (2022) proposed a conceptual framework consolidating diverse digital technologies into a unified ecosystem. However, their framework remained entirely conceptual without empirical testing. This gap between conceptual design and functional deployment represents a primary justification for this project's emphasis on delivering a tested, working prototype enhanced with practical AI and offline Mesh capabilities.2.5 Security Management in Educational InstitutionsUniversities present a uniquely complex security environment due to their open character and diverse populations. Wodi and Wordu (2024) concluded that digitalisation produces measurable improvements in safety outcomes by enabling faster threat detection, though they identified funding and privacy as persistent barriers. Inyang (2025) found that individual ICT tools produce limited impact when not integrated into a coherent operational framework. Ogbu and Excellence (2025) provided directly relevant empirical evidence, finding that emergency alert systems and mobile security applications are among the most underutilised ICT tools in Nigerian universities. The S.A.F.E system is designed precisely to address this documented adoption gap by ensuring the system is highly usable, intelligent, and resilient.2.6 Communication Gap in Security SystemsEffective communication is a foundational pillar of security management. Suleiman and Akanbi (2024) identified a pervasive communication gap as a primary structural factor undermining campus security, noting that negative student perceptions and the absence of accessible reporting channels actively discourage incident reporting. Eke and Adeyemi (2025) found that mobile applications possess substantial potential to lower barriers to incident engagement, though challenges like digital illiteracy and inconsistent internet access constrain this potential. These findings directly informed the S.A.F.E system's priority on an intuitive, low-barrier UI, the integration of Mesh Networking to bypass internet dependency, and the AI classification engine that reduces the cognitive burden of incident categorisation.2.7 Emergency Response Systems and TechnologiesThe latency between incident occurrence and effective intervention is directly correlated with human safety outcomes. Yusuf, Oniye, and Abdulrasaq (2022) produced strong evidence that accessible alert mechanisms produce significant reductions in incident severity by enabling faster mobilisation. Kini, Nayak, Sreekantha, Shaivi, Abishek, and Shetty (2024) achieved GPS update refresh rates of under two seconds in their safety management system, providing a rigorous empirical standard for real-time performance. Mayowa, Otuka, Ajienka, and Nwajana (2025) demonstrated the technical feasibility of mobile-first, citizen-driven security reporting in Nigeria using Agile methodology. Nandom, Adda, Adi, and Benjamin (2022) developed an IoT-based crime reporting system using ReactJS and Firebase, demonstrating the viability of distributed architectures. Collectively, these studies ground the emergency alert, real-time tracking, and AI-enhanced incident reporting components of the S.A.F.E system.2.8 Challenges of Implementing ICT in SecurityMohammed (2024) identified structural impediments to ICT deployment: chronic underfunding, high capital costs, poor maintenance culture, and power instability. Akpam and Edet (2024) identified budget constraints and data privacy concerns as principal barriers. Guanah (2024) found that the media has failed to educate the public about security technologies, contributing to low awareness. Dada, Mohammed, and Mallam (2025) found very low uptake rates of advanced technologies due to high costs and technical barriers. The S.A.F.E system addresses these constraints by minimising infrastructure dependencies, optimising for low-bandwidth environments, utilising decentralised Mesh Networking to ensure functionality even when primary networks fail, and deploying a lightweight, quantised AI model suitable for mobile-class hardware.2.9 Identified Research GapsA critical synthesis of the literature reveals substantive gaps:Theory-Practice Disconnect: Existing studies remain at the level of conceptual frameworks without producing functional implementations (Mustapha et al., 2022).Absence of Real-Time Reporting Capabilities: Most platforms lack integrated real-time functionalities (Ogbu &amp; Excellence, 2025).Fragmentation of Security Technology: Current deployments are isolated and lack interoperability (Akpam &amp; Edet, 2024).Inadequate User-Centred Design: Poor usability contributes to low adoption and underreporting (Suleiman &amp; Akanbi, 2024).Lack of Fault-Tolerance and Intelligence: Existing local systems lack offline capabilities (Mesh) and intelligent triage (AI), rendering them vulnerable to network outages and alert fatigue.No Implemented System at the Study Institution: No functional platform exists at Kwara State University.2.10 Justification of the Proposed SystemThe literature firmly justifies the S.A.F.E system. The one-touch emergency alert responds to Yusuf et al. (2022). The real-time mobile interface addresses Ogbu and Excellence (2025). The GPS tagging is grounded in Abdullahi and Muhammad (2025). The two-way communication responds to Suleiman and Akanbi (2024). architecture extending the standard three-tier client-server pattern with a dedicated Edge Intelligence and Mesh Layer:Presentation Tier (Client Layer): React Native mobile application (students and staff) and React.js web application (security administrators). React Native was selected for its cross-platform compatibility, enabling a single codebase to serve both Android and iOS users.Logic Tier (Application Layer): Node.js with Express.js framework manages all application logic, processes client requests, enforces business rules, handles authentication, and coordinates real-time communication via Socket.io WebSocket technology.Data Tier (Database Layer): MySQL 8 relational database, accessed through the Sequelize ORM, manages all persistent data including user profiles, incident records, AI classification outputs, Mesh packet logs, and communication logs.Edge Intelligence and Mesh Layer: TensorFlow Lite manages on-device AI inference for incident classification and severity scoring. The OS-native Nearby Connections API (Android) and Multipeer Connectivity Framework (iOS), abstracted via a React Native bridge, manage the Bluetooth Low Energy and Wi-Fi Direct Mesh Networking protocol. A Python-based backend cron job runs nightly DBSCAN clustering on historical incident geotags to generate the predictive heatmap pushed to the admin dashboard.3.5.1 Real-Time Communication ArchitectureReal-time push notifications and two-way messaging are implemented using Socket.io, integrated with the Node.js backend. Mesh alerts that arrive at a gateway node are forwarded to the backend via a dedicated /api/mesh/sync REST endpoint and then broadcast to connected admin dashboard clients via the mesh_alert_synced Socket.io event, ensuring administrators receive offline-originated alerts without manual refresh.3.6 System Modules3.6.1 User Authentication ModuleThis module manages registration, login, and JWT-based authentication for all system users. Users register using institutional email addresses and are assigned role-based access profiles: Standard User (students and staff) or Security Administrator. A custom Express middleware function verifies token validity and role permissions on every protected route.3.6.2 Incident Reporting ModuleThe primary reporting interface. Users select an incident category, enter a free-text description, and may optionally attach multimedia evidence. Upon submission, the on-device DistilBERT model (quantised to INT8 via TensorFlow Lite) analyses the description text, suggests a category, and assigns a severity score (0–100). The score and suggestion are returned to the user via the ai_classification_ready Socket.io event and also stored with the incident record. This reduces user friction and improves data consistency for downstream analytics.3.6.3 Emergency Alert ModuleThis module provides a prominent one-touch emergency alert button accessible from the home screen without navigation. On activation, the application first attempts HTTPS transmission. If the request times out — indicating no internet — the Mesh protocol activates: the alert payload (User ID, GPS coordinates, timestamp, severity) is AES-256 encrypted and broadcast via BLE. Nearby S.A.F.E devices receive the packet, decrement a Time-To-Live (TTL) counter, and rebroadcast. Once a device with internet access receives the packet, it acts as a gateway node and forwards the alert to the backend via /api/mesh/sync. An SMS fallback is also integrated: if no administrator acknowledges an alert within fifteen seconds, an automated SMS is dispatched to the security control line.3.6.4 GPS Location ModuleIntegrates with the device's native GPS via the Expo Location API to automatically capture and attach geographic coordinates to all submitted incident reports and emergency alerts. On the administrative dashboard, React-Leaflet renders each incident and alert as a colour-coded marker — red for unresolved alerts, amber for in-progress, green for resolved — enabling hotspot identification and patrol prioritisation.3.6.5 Two-Way Communication ModuleEach incident record is associated with a dedicated message thread persisted in the Messages table. Real-time delivery is achieved via Socket.io rooms keyed by incident ID. Offline message history is retrieved via a REST endpoint on reconnection, ensuring no communication is lost.3.6.6 Administrative Dashboard ModuleA React.js single-page web application. Core features include: a real-time incident feed with AI severity scores; an interactive campus map with incident markers and AI-generated predictive heatmap; incident management tools for assignment, status update, and internal notes; an AI Analytics Panel displaying incident volume over time, category-wise distribution, and geographic hotspot visualisation generated by the DBSCAN cron job; and a user management console for account administration.3.7 Database DesignThe system database is structured around the following principal entities:Users: Stores user profile data including name, institutional email, password hash, role, and registration date.Incidents: Stores all incident reports including category, description, GPS coordinates, status, AI severity score, AI category suggestion, and assigned officer.Alerts: Stores emergency alert events including GPS coordinates, transmission mode (HTTPS or Mesh), and resolution status.MeshPackets: Stores Mesh relay logs including encrypted payload, TTL, origin and relay device IDs, and server synchronisation status.Messages: Stores communication log entries including sender, content, and timestamp.Notifications: Stores push notification records including recipient, content, and delivery status.Relational integrity constraints and indexed foreign keys are defined to ensure data consistency and to optimise query performance for real-time operations.3.8 User Interface Design PrinciplesThe user interface is designed in accordance with user-centred design principles calibrated to diverse digital literacy levels: minimal navigation depth (core actions within 1–2 taps); clear visual hierarchy; consistent visual and haptic feedback; accessible language; and AI-assisted category suggestion to reduce reporting effort. During registration, users supply their institutional email address, full name, and password; passwords are hashed using bcrypt before persistence, and each user is assigned a role attribute of either standard_user or security_admin. Upon successful login, the server issues a signed JWT containing the user's identifier and role, which the client stores securely and attaches to the Authorization header of subsequent requests. A custom Express middleware function verifies token validity and role permissions on every protected route.4.4.2 Incident Reporting Module with AI ClassificationThe incident reporting module is implemented as a guided, multi-step form on the mobile application. Users select an incident category from a predefined list, enter a free-text description, and may optionally attach multimedia evidence using the Expo ImagePicker and AV APIs. Upon submission, the description text is first passed to the on-device DistilBERT model via TensorFlow Lite. The model — pre-trained on incident-related text and quantised to INT8 for mobile-class hardware — extracts semantic features from the text and outputs a category suggestion and a severity score between 0 and 100. This result is displayed to the user before final submission, allowing them to confirm or override the AI suggestion. The confirmed report, AI outputs, and GPS coordinates are then transmitted to /api/incidents. The backend persists the full record and emits a new_incident Socket.io event — carrying the AI severity score — to all connected administrator clients.4.4.3 Emergency Alert Module with Mesh Networking FallbackThe emergency alert feature is implemented as a fixed, prominently coloured floating action button on the home screen. On activation, the application immediately captures the user's current GPS coordinates and attempts HTTPS transmission to the backend. If the request times out within a configurable threshold — indicating no internet — the Mesh protocol is activated automatically without requiring further user input.In Mesh mode, the alert payload (User ID, GPS coordinates, timestamp, severity classification) is serialised and AES-256 encrypted. The application broadcasts the packet via Bluetooth Low Energy using the platform's Nearby Connections (Android) or Multipeer Connectivity (iOS) API. Nearby devices running the S.A.F.E application receive the packet, decrement the Time-To-Live (TTL) counter — initialised to 5 — and rebroadcast it if TTL is greater than zero. When a relay device regains internet connectivity, it detects the queued Mesh packet and forwards it to the backend via the /api/mesh/sync endpoint. The backend persists the packet to the MeshPackets table and emits a mesh_alert_synced Socket.io event to the admin dashboard. In testing, a three-node relay chain delivered the alert to the server within 4.2 seconds under simulated dead-zone conditions.An SMS fallback is also active in parallel: if no administrator acknowledgement is received within fifteen seconds of alert transmission — via either HTTPS or Mesh sync — the system automatically dispatches an SMS notification containing the user's name and last-known GPS coordinates to the designated security control line.4.4.4 GPS Location ModuleGeolocation functionality was implemented using the Expo Location API. On first use, the application requests foreground location permission; once granted, device coordinates are retrieved at configured accuracy and attached automatically to incident reports and emergency alerts at the point of submission. On the administrative dashboard, React-Leaflet renders each incident and alert as a colour-coded marker — red for unresolved alerts, amber for in-progress incidents, green for resolved cases. The AI-generated predictive heatmap is rendered as a semi-transparent GeoJSON overlay on the same map.4.4.5 Two-Way Communication ModuleEach incident record is associated with a dedicated message thread persisted in the Messages table and linked via foreign key. Real-time delivery is achieved by having both the reporting user's client and the assigned administrator's client join a Socket.io room identified by the incident's unique identifier. Messages sent within that room are broadcast instantly to all participants and simultaneously written to the database, creating a permanent, timestamped, and auditable communication record. For users offline at the time a message is sent, the complete message history is retrieved via a REST endpoint on reconnection.4.4.6 Administrative Dashboard Module with AI Analytics PanelThe administrative dashboard was implemented as a React.js single-page web application. Its principal interface presents: a real-time incident feed — populated via a persistent Socket.io connection, updating without page refresh — with each entry displaying the AI-assigned severity score and category suggestion; an interactive campus map (React-Leaflet) with colour-coded incident markers and the DBSCAN-derived predictive heatmap overlay; incident management tools for assignment, status update (Received, In Progress, Resolved), and internal case notes; and an AI Analytics Panel built using the Recharts library, presenting incident volume over time, category-wise distribution, and geographic hotspot concentrations. A user management console, restricted to super-administrator accounts, allows user creation, deactivation, and role assignment.4.5 Database ImplementationThe database schema specified in Section 3.7 was implemented in MySQL 8 using Sequelize models.
